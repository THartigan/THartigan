L12 - Feedback Processes in Star Formation

  • Theories of gas cooling and gravitational collapse predict around 80% of baryons should have been converted into stars. However, observations show only around 4% conversion rate.
    • Star formation is thought to be suppressed by negative feedback processes associated with star formation and accretion onto SHMBs.

Stellar Effects

Stellar Wind

  • Stars lose mass through stellar winds.
    • Post-main sequence stars near the end of their lives produce winds ejecting large quantities of mass ∼M˙>10−3M⊙yr−1\sim \dot M >10^{-3}M_\odot \text{yr}^{-1}∼M˙>10−3M⊙​yr−1﻿ with relatively low velocity ∼<10 km s−1\sim<10\text{ km s}^{-1}∼<10 km s−1﻿.
      • Examples are red giants, super giants, and asymptotic giant branch stars. These winds are likely driven by radiation pressure on dust condensing in the upper stellar atmosphere.
    • Massive O and B stars produce stellar winds with much lower mass loss rates ∼M˙<10−6M⊙ yr−1\sim \dot M < 10^{-6}M_\odot \text{ yr}^{-1}∼M˙<10−6M⊙​ yr−1﻿ but very high velocities ∼v>1000−2000 km s−1\sim v>1000-2000\text{ km s}^{-1}∼v>1000−2000 km s−1﻿.
      • These winds are driven by radiation pressure on heavy element (carbon & nitrogen) resonance absorption lines, provide significant feedback and drive shocks with a two-shock structure into the ISM.
        • The inner shock is generated where the initial outflow particles are thermalised to produce an X-ray emitting plasma at around 106 K10^6\text{ K}106 K﻿. This region of hot, high-pressure gas then expands into the surrounding gas.
        • If the surrounding gas is sufficiently dense, then the swept up gas radiatively cools faster than the inner shock, forming a thin, dense, surrounding shell.
        • Around 20% of stellar wind kinetic energy is imparted into the kinetic energy of the ISM.
      • Winds greatly effect the end of life behaviour of stars, e.g. resulting in formation of white dwarfs rather than supernovae as so much mass has been lost.
      • Notably, massive O-B stars provide additional feedback due to their high luminosities, giving both photoionisation (and therefore heating) of the ISM and sufficient radiation pressure onto ISM dust to eject gas from a galaxy.
  • As efficient cooling requires radiation from molecules and dust, which are often destroyed by the heating of shocks and photoionisation, these processes reduce the susceptibility of ISM gas to collapse and fragmentation. This therefore decreases the star formation rate.

Supernovae, Starbursts and Ultraluminous Galaxies

  • Supernovae have a much stronger feedback effect than stellar winds in galaxies producing very massive stars of >8M⊙>8M_\odot>8M⊙​﻿, as they each result in metallicity enrichment, and release around 1043−1044 J10^{43}-10^{44}\text{ J}1043−1044 J﻿, and 1M⊙1M_\odot1M⊙​﻿ of material back to the ISM.
  • A starburst galaxy can be considered as one in which the star formation rate will exhaust all available gas in a period much shorter than the Hubble time
    • M82 is a nuclear starburst with the associated supernovae driving galactic-scale outflow of hot gas - easily visible in an X-ray image.
    • Ultraluminous infrared galaxies (ULIRGs) are starburst galaxies with far-infrared luminosities greater than 1012L⊙10^{12} L_\odot 1012L⊙​﻿ and star formation rates ∼50−1000M⊙yr−1\sim 50-1000 M_\odot \text{yr}^{-1}∼50−1000M⊙​yr−1﻿.
  • The galactic winds produced by SN explosions and radiation pressure from young O-B stars expel gas out of the galaxy, reducing fuel for star formation and therefore reducing the star formation rate.
    • This is particularly important for low-mass galaxies with a sufficiently shallow potential well to retain the gas.
    • Effects like this and this are called global stellar feedback.

Feedback from Supermassive Accreting Black Holes

  • The mass of SMBHs is proportional to that of the centroid of their galaxy with MBH∼10−3MsphM_\text{BH}\sim 10^{-3}M_\text{sph}MBH​∼10−3Msph​﻿, and viscosity within a SMBH accretion disk converts gravitational energy into thermal energy, heating the disk to around 105 K10^5 \text{ K}105 K﻿ and causing significant Black-Body UV radiation.
    • High luminosity SMHBs are AGNs, and can photoionise gas clouds over large galactic regions. Quasars are the most extreme AGNs with luminosities >1012L⊙>10^{12}L_\odot>1012L⊙​﻿, such that they outshine the rest of the galaxies. Hence, they appear star-like and are called Quasi Stellar Objects, QSOs.

        • Radio-mode feedback, where a significantly sub-Eddington rate BH produces powerful radio jets, injecting energy into the intergalactic and inter-cluster medium, preventing it from cooling, and therefore preventing it from feeding the central galaxy with gas.
      • These are thought to act in sequence, with the quasar mode clearing a galaxy of gas and the radio-mode preventing new galactic gas accretion, preventing star formation.
        • The galaxy then becomes old elliptical.
