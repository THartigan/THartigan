Interacting Galaxies

  • Many galaxies interact with lower-mass companions. These companions could possibly cause extremely well-defined spiral arms, through the indiction of tidal density waves (e.g. M51).
    • A tidal tail like that seen on the left below is a direct indication of strong gravitational interaction.
    • Galactic collisions can lead to high rates of star formation and hard X-ray emission.
      • Timescales for these collisions are predicted very well by the simple analysis in (4).
  • Simulations show that even just a perturbation of a galaxy by another passing nearby results in significant changes;
    • In the above, we see that spiral density waves are induced, resulting in shocks and cloud-collisions (causing a loss of angular momentum and inward gas flow) which dissipate the kinetic energy of the cloud population. This results in gas accumulation on the inner Lindblad resonance.
      • This high gas density then drives a burst of star formation.
      • After another period of time, the gas dissipates more kinetic energy and accumulates in the galactic core, driving a second burst of star formation.
        • Some of this gas accumulated in the core can also accrete onto the SMBH causing AGN activity.
    • These effects are observed in ULIRGs, which invariably show signs of interactions and mergers.
  • Formation of a stellar bar can often be triggered by mild galaxy interactions.
    • Gas on circular orbits then encounters the bar at supersonic speeds, resulting in shocks which dissipate kinetic energy and cause gas to accumulate in the bar.
    • Orbits on the bar are also highly elongated, bringing gas and stars to the nucleus, fuelling nuclear processes.
