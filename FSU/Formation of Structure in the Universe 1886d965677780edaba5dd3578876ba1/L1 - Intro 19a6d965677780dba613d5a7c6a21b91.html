Universe Evolution

The First 3 Minutes

  • Current models suggest the universe began in a Big Bang followed by a period of exponential inflation from 10−36 s10^{-36}\text{ s}10−36 s﻿ to 10−32 s10^{-32}\text{ s}10−32 s﻿.
    • This is favoured, as it rectifies both the flatness and causal connectedness problems in cosmology.
    • It also correctly predicts the statistical fluctuations seen in the universe by assuming that inflation caused quantum fluctuations in the inflation field to be stretched to macroscopic scales.
  • After inflation, universe density and temperature (and hence average particle energy) continue to fall. Around 1μs1\mu s1μs﻿, quarks and gluons combine to form baryons like protons and neutrons.
    • Protons and neutrons then quickly fuse into nuclei like deuterium, helium and lithium before all of the neutrons decay.
  • After a few minutes, the universe is composed of radiation (photons + neutrinos), baryonic matter, dark matter and dark energy (which dominates the mass/energy budget).

Formation of the First Stars and Galaxies

  • As the universe expands, it continues to cool and over-dense regions dominated by dark matter grow from the initial perturbation.
    • Eventually around 370 kyr370 \text{ kyr}370 kyr﻿ after the BB, mater and radiation decouple, allowing the formation of neutral elements via e.g. e+p→H+γe+p\rightarrow H+\gammae+p→H+γ﻿, and hence increasing the transparency of the universe.
      • This is recombination - the emitted photons form the modern day CMB, carrying an imprint of the initial structure of the universe.
  • After recombination, there are no sources of light, resulting in the ‘Dark Ages’.
    • During the dark ages, dark matter continues to collect in the over-dense regions, eventually becoming self-gravitating and sufficiently dense to decouple from the Hubble flow (motion due to solely the expansion of the universe).
    • These dark matter potential wells then gravitate towards each other and merge, forming larger structures which eventually attract baryonic matter.
      • Baryonic matter at the centre of these potential wells eventually cools sufficiently to form the first luminous objects.
  • This heating and cooling of gas in a gravitational field results in a multi-phase medium where cool clouds exist in equilibrium with hotter, low-density phases.
    • The densest regions can cool sufficiently fast to be unstable to gravitational collapse. These collapse processes then fragment the baryonic gas clouds and result in the formation of protostars as the collapsing gas heats up. This is cosmic dawn.
    • As the parent clouds have angular momentum, the formation of protostars also forms an associated protostellar disk, which eventually enables planet formation.

Reionisation

  • Reionisation then occurs because the photons emitted from the first stars start to ionise the neutral hydrogen formed at recombination.
    • As the neutral hydrogen always absorbs CMB photons, we can observe the CMB at different epochs to measure the fraction of neutral hydrogen with time. Measurements suggest ionisation of neutral hydrogen was completed by around 1Gyr1 \text{Gyr}1Gyr﻿ after the BB.
  • Studying this reionisation process leads to conclusions that;
    • Structure forms hierarchically with dark matter halos seeding baryonic matter density.
    • This provides a large amount of dense gas, allowing for very fast early galaxy evolution.
    • A distribution of galaxies with different masses is formed.
  • Note that the most distant confirmed galaxy observation is at z=14.18z=14.18z=14.18﻿, or ∼280 Myr\sim 280 \text{ Myr}∼280 Myr﻿ after the BB.
