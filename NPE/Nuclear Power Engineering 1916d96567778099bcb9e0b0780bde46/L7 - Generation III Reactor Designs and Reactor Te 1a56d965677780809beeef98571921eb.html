Generation III Reactor Designs

Advanced Passive Pressurised Water Reactors

  • Include passive safety features like the natural circulation of air, water and steam.
    • For example, the flow of coolant water is gravity-driven, heat transfer is based on conduction and convection, and fail-safe valves are used.
    • The safety features for an AP600 reactor are shown in the figure above. If a loss of coolant accident (LOCA) occurs, the following occur automatically;
      1. All control rods are inserted on detection of the LOCA.
      1. The gravity-driven core makeup tank (CMT) injects water into the pressure vessel.
      1. The passive residual heat removal (PRHR) system removes heat automatically by natural circulation.
      1. More water is injected into the pressure vessel by the pressure-driven accumulator injection system.
      1. When the primary circuit pressure reaches atmospheric pressure, the automatic depressurisation (ADS) valves open, allowing the large volume of water in the in-containment refuelling water storage tank (IRWST) to flow into the pressure vessel under gravity (5).
  • Examples include the Westinghouse AP600/AP1000 and European Pressurised Reactors (EPR). Hinkley Point C is an EPR.
    • These designs are simpler than older PWRs with far fewer large pumps, heat exchangers, valves, pipes and cabling.
    • Operate at lower power densities than older PWRs, reducing temperature gradients, decreased material degradation, improved fuel longevity and an increased margin to the critical heat flux at which coolant boiling becomes unstable.
    • These reactors also allow modular plant construction - enabling centralised manufacturing and assembly before shipment to a site and allowing for construction parallelisation (on site and at the manufacturing facility).

Pebble Bed Modular Reactor

  • This is a helium-cooled, graphite-moderated, high-temperature reactor (HTR) with a 6m diameter 20m high pressure vessel lined by 1m of graphite which acts as a reflector and a passive heat transfer medium.
    • PBMRs use silicon carbide and pyrolytic carbon coated particles of 8% enriched UO2UO_2UO2​﻿ encased in a graphite sphere.
      • Around 1/3 of the spheres are pure graphite, acting as a moderator.
    • The helium coolant enters the reactor at around 84 bar and 500∘C500^\circ C500∘C﻿, leaving at the bottom around 900∘C900^\circ C900∘C﻿.
      • The output gases are then used to drive two compressor turbines and a generator turbine. The high output temperature allows thermal efficiencies around 50%50\%50%﻿.
    • PBMRs can also be refuelled online, with pebbles circulated from the bottom to the top of the core 10 times on average over the course of around 3 years, with spent pebbles then being replaced. The graphite spheres last around 10 years.
  • The unique safety features include;
    • The silicon carbide coating surrounding each uranium fuel particle which acts as a pressure vessel, preventing the release of fission products during operation.
    • The use of graphite allows the pebble to remain stable up to 2800∘C2800^\circ C2800∘C﻿, above the maximum predicted temperature during a depressurised loss of coolant accident (DLOCA).
    • Use of inert helium, which doesn’t react with the graphite or metallic core components and has very small absorption cross-section, so its presence or lack thereof has little effect on reactivity.
    • The low power density, high thermal inertia of the core and conductivity of graphite maintain DLOCA temperatures below 1600∘C1600^\circ C1600∘C﻿.
    • Continuous fuelling ensures there is no large excess reactivity in the core.

Accelerator Driven Subcritical Reactors

  • These offer potential advantages given they could generate significant energy and incinerate high-level waste from conventional reactors by transmutation.
    • Furthermore, ADSRs are intrinsically safe because they operate below criticality (keff∈[0.94,0.98]k_{eff}\in [0.94, 0.98]keff​∈[0.94,0.98]﻿), could potentially breed fissile material, and can operate with thorium fuel.
      • The use of thorium fuel is especially advantageous, as it is 3-4 times more abundant than uranium, doesn’t require enrichment before use in reactors (some fissile starter material is required), and the fuel cycle produces both less plutonium and less high-level waste (easier long-term disposal).
    • As ADSRs operate with keff<1k_{eff}<1keff​<1﻿, power generation can only be sustained if additional neutrons are provided. This is achieved by neutron spallation where a heavy metal target is bombarded by a high power proton beam. A 1 GeV1 \text{ GeV}1 GeV﻿ proton beam produces around 25 neutrons per proton.
      • Without the accelerator providing spallation neutrons, the fission reaction dies out. However, the heat arising from the decay of fission products must still be removed during this cooldown.
  • Most ADSR concepts based on thorium have an initial load of 232Th{}^{232}Th232Th﻿ and fissile 235U{}^{235}U235U﻿ or 239Pu{}^{239}Pu239Pu﻿.
    • 233U{}^{233}U233U﻿ can then be bred from 232Th{}^{232}Th232Th﻿ by neutron capture, and power generated by fissioning this 233Pu{}^{233}Pu233Pu﻿ and 235U{}^{235}U235U﻿/ 239Pu{}^{239}Pu239Pu﻿ using both the spallation and fission neutrons.
    • Excess neutrons could then be used to transmute long-life nuclear waste.
