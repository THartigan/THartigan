Binding Energy

  • The energy required to separate all elements within a nucleus by an infinite distance. Has contributions from;
    • Nuclear force binding nucleons together at short range.
    • Coulomb repulsion results in a potential energy contribution ∝1/r\propto 1/r∝1/r﻿ at long range, balancing the strong repulsion.
  • In heavy nuclei, the strong nuclear force is insufficient to counteract Coulomb repulsion (nuclei may be larger than the range of the strong nuclear force), so heavy nuclei require more neutrons to hold the nuclei together.
    • In small nuclei, n/p∼1n/p\sim 1n/p∼1﻿.
    • The most stable nuclides have the highest binding energy per nucleon.

Radioactive Decay

  • Nuclei in excited states release EM radiation as γ\gammaγ﻿ photons.
  • Nuclei can also decay to other nuclei with higher binding energy per nucleon. Generally via;
    • α\alphaα﻿-decay, releasing a He4He^4He4﻿ nucleus (most common in nuclei heavier than PbPbPb﻿).
      • Note how the He4He^4He4﻿ nucleus is particularly stable in terms of binding energy per nucleon.
    • β−\beta^-β−﻿-decay, releasing an electron (nuclei with an excess of neutrons, turning the neutron into a proton)
    • β+\beta^+β+﻿-decay, releasing a positron (nuclei with an excess of protons, turning the proton into a neutron)
    • Electron capture (EC), occurs when low energy electrons are absorbed into the nucleus, turning a proton into a neutron.
      • Occurs when proton and electron wave-functions have sufficient overlap.
    • Neutron emission
    • Internal conversion (by complex magnetic interactions)
    • Spontaneous fission (splitting into two components, α\alphaα﻿-decay is a very asymmetric version of spontaneous fission).

Fission

  • Fission processes often require an initial activation energy, as fission requires deforming the spherical nucleus (which minimises the surface term in the SEMF) (1).
    • This deformation increases the surface area term, but decreases the Coulomb term. Initially, the surface term wins, increasing the overall potential energy (BBB﻿ decreases) (2).
    • As deformation increases further, the potential energy decreases due to much decreased Coulomb repulsion and the nucleus separates into two parts, increasing BBB﻿ (3).
    • Note that for Z>115Z>115Z>115﻿, the fission barrier height is ∼0\sim0∼0﻿.
  • Fission barriers can be overcome either by tunnelling through the barrier (α\alphaα﻿-decay) or by the absorption of an energetic particle, often a neutron, as it doesn’t experience a Coulomb barrier.
    • Nuclei with an even number of protons or neutrons or both tend to be more stable due to spin stabilisation.
    • Adding an additional neutron to a nucleus with an even number of neutrons therefore tends to release less energy than adding an additional neutron to a nucleus with an odd number of neutrons;
      • The activation energy for fission of 239U{}^{239}U239U﻿, is 6.6 MeV6.6 \text{ MeV}6.6 MeV﻿, so cannot occur unless the absorbed neutron has kinetic energy >1.8 MeV>1.8 \text{ MeV}>1.8 MeV﻿.
        • Nuclides like that which will undergo fission only if hit by high energy neutrons are called fissionable.
        • Note that double β−\beta^-β−﻿ decay from 239U{}^{239}U239U﻿ produces 239Pu{}^{239}Pu239Pu﻿.
      • The activation energy for fission of 236U{}^{236}U236U﻿ is 6.2 MeV6.2\text{ MeV}6.2 MeV﻿, so the second process causes 236U{}^{236}U236U﻿ to fission regardless of incident neutron energy.
        • Nuclides like this which will undergo fission when hit by neutrons of any energy are called fissile.
  • Fission is asymmetric, with product masses becoming more symmetric with increased incident neutron energy.
