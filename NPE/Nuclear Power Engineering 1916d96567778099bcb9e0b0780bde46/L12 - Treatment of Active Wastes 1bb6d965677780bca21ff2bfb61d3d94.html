L12 - Treatment of Active Wastes

Classification and Transport of Waste

Classification

  • In the UK, waste is categorised as;
    • Low level (< 4 GBq/tonne α\alphaα﻿ and < 12 GBq/tonne βγ\beta\gammaβγ﻿).
      • This comes from lab equipment, clothes and building materials.
      • Often just sealed inside a metal container ~4 Mm34\text{ Mm}^34 Mm3﻿.
    • Intermediate level (>>>﻿LLW, but not heat generating).
      • This includes power station parts, storage vessels and fuel sludge.
      • Encased in concrete or sealed in a metal container ~0.3 Mm30.3\text{ Mm}^30.3 Mm3﻿.
    • High level (>>>﻿LLW and heat generating).
      • Spent fuel.
      • Vitrified and sealed inside a stainless steel or copper flask ~ 1000 m31000 \text{ m}^31000 m3﻿.
    • The UK also allows wastes giving a public dose below 0.3 MsV/yr to be disposed of in designated nuclear landfill sites.
      • The average doses in such cases is normally below 0.02 mSv/yr.
  • The IAEA categorise waste as;

    1.0 - Exempt waste with a public dose < 0.01 mSv

    2.0 - Low and intermediate (LILW) with a public dose above 0.01 mSv and thermal power < 2 kWm−22\text{ kWm}^{-2}2 kWm−2﻿.

    2.1 - Short lived LILW (LILW-SL) with α<400\alpha<400α<400﻿ Bq / gram per package.

    2.2 - Long lived LILW (LILW-LL) with α>400\alpha>400α>400﻿ Bq / gram per package

    3.0 - High level waste (HLW) with α>400\alpha > 400α>400﻿ Bq / gram per package and thermal power >2 kWm−2> 2\text{ kWm}^{-2}>2 kWm−2﻿.

Transport

  • Waste is transported in packages with three tiers of protection;
    • Type A - Withstand minor accidents, used for medium-activity materials (medical or industrial radioisotopes).
    • Type B - For transporting HLW and used fuel. These are robust and very secure, with gamma and neutron shielding even under extreme conditions.
    • Type C - Even greater protections than type B, used for plutonium.

Ion Exchange

  • Active ions, especially strontium, can be substituted out of compounds for more reactive group 1 metals like calcium or sodium.
    • The ion exchange media (which collects the radioactive atoms) can be either organic or inorganic.
      • Inorganic media is encapsulated in concrete and organic media is chemically treated before encapsulation.
    • This is implemented in Site Ion Exchange Plants (SIXEP), which first use sand filters to remove solid wastes from the sludge, then correct the PH of the sludge and finally let the sludge through two ion exchange vessels (3 available to allow for permuting which are active).
  • This generally achieves DF~10-100, but is only possible with liquids.

Chemical Treatment

  • Used to neutralise acidic or alkaline wastes to reduce corrosion and handling risks.
  • It is also possible to form insoluble compounds which “drag down” radionuclides from the solution, e.g. Fe or Al hydroxides can pull Cs or Sr ions into the solid phase for removal. (Ion exchange)
  • Previously discussed solvent extraction is also an example of chemical treatment.
    • This is used primarily for fuel production and waste reprocessing.

Evaporation

  • Enables very high DFs up to 10610^6106﻿, and have a very large energy cost.
  • They are also difficult to maintain, with cleaning resulting in a high operator dose.
    • Evaporation is therefore only used when there is no alternative.

Storage

  • Low level waste such as generate site waste is packed into drums, compacted, placed in shipping containers, and stored in an engineered landfill site.
  • Solid wastes and sludges can also be stored by encapsulation in either cement, bitumen or polymers.
    • This is more difficult with organic wastes, as the calcium in the cement can cause ion exchange. To mitigate this, ion exchange is now used prior to encapsulation.
    • This process is accomplished using one of the three method below;
  • Vitrification is used for highly active, long half-life wastes.
    • This is expensive, but ensures suitability for long-term storage and cooling.
      • The vitrification process also produces off-gases which require further processing.
    • Synroc processes are also gaining traction.
  • In theory, transmutation can be used to convert highly active wastes into shorter half-life isotopes by bombarding them with neutrons.
