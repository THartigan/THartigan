L11 - Nuclear Fuel Reprocessing

Reprocessing

  • Spent fuel still contains ~96% U with ~0.85% U-235 and ~0.9% Pu. Reprocessing this fuel allows 25% more energy to be obtained from the same Uranium.
    • Reprocessing also separates the highly active fission products, significantly reducing the volume of high level waste.
    • Reprocessing also recovers Pu which can then be used in MOX fuel and fast reactors.

Reusing Uranium

  • Burnt fuel contains less than 1% U-235, but between 0.4-0.6% U-236, which is a strong neutron absorber, and traces of U-232.
    • These U-232 traces gives rise to Th-228 and other daughters, which are strong γ\gammaγ﻿-emitters, making handling difficult.
      • These U-232 decay products built up with time, so it is optimal to quickly enrich and re-fabricate fuel once reprocessed.
    • U-232 does, however gain a neutron in a reactor to become fissile U-233.
    • A further 0.3% U-235 is required to overcome an extra 1% U-236 concentration, therefore increasing SWUs required by 26% compared to a fresh feed.
    • Any plutonium material over 202020﻿ GWd/tonne burn-up is considered reactor grade and significantly different from weapons grade plutonium.
      • Notably, burn-up increases with decreases fissile Pu, as a longer fuel cycle allows more Pu and Pu products to be fissioned.

Method

  • Prior to reprocessing, fuel is removed from the reactor, cooled at the power station for a few years, transported to the reprocessing plant and then stored at the reprocessing plant. The following method is then followed for reprocessing;
  • First, the fuel pins are chopped using a hydraulic shear, before being transferred to the dissolver.
  • Within the dissolver, the spent fuel is dissolved by hot nitric acid.
    • This produces off-gasses containing NOxNO_xNOx​﻿, KrKrKr﻿, XeXeXe﻿, III﻿, H3H_3H3​﻿ and C-14.
      • The NOxNO_xNOx​﻿ and III﻿ are removed by water and caustic scrubbing (neutralisation with NaOH).
      • The C-14 is precipitated as barium carbonate and stored.
      • The gases are discharged to the environment following electrostatic precipitation.
    • This also leaves over the slightly contaminated fuel pin hulls.
      • These are then encapsulated in cement and stored in 500L drums, ready to be stored in a deep geological repository.
  • Primary separation then utilises solvent extraction, exploiting how different compounds are much more soluble in certain solvents.
    • U and Pu are soluble in a 30% solution of tri-butyl phosphate (TBP) in odourless kerosene (TBP/OK), whilst most fission products are insoluble, so remain the the aqueous phase.
      • Traces of Zn, Nb and Ru are also soluble in TBP.
    • This separation occurs in a mixer settler, with a mixing section adjacent to a settling area, from which the most settled organic and aqueous phase components are removed. The aqueous phase is then a highly active liquor (HAL).
      • This HAL contains 99.9% of the fission products and is very highly radioactive and heat generating.
      • It is concentrated by evaporation and then stored in water cooled tanks before vitrification for long-term storage.
  • Pu(III) is not soluble in TBP/OK, so reducing the Pu(IV) found in the nitric acid phase returns the Pu to the aqueous stream.
    • The two phases can then be separated again using a mixer separator, giving a U rich organic phase and a Pu rich aqueous phase (the two steps above are summarised in the figure below);
    • This process produces low and medium active liquid wastes, off-gases, low activity solid wastes and spent solvent.
      • The separation was conducted using mixer settlers for magnox fuels - they are simple, but THORP (thermal oxide reprocessing plants) now use pulsed columns.
        • Pulsed columns are vertical columns packed with sieve trays (small holes), such that when the fluid is pulsed up and down, the lighter (organic) phase migrates to the top and the heavier (aqueous) phase, to the base.
        • Pulsed columns are preferred for high radiation levels as they ensure a criticality safe geometry.
  • Purification and finishing then occurs for Pu / U;
    • For Pu purification, we first wash the stream with clean solvent, then re-oxidise to Pu(IV) using NOxNO_xNOx​﻿/ Air, and finally wash with TBP/OK to put back into the organic phase.
      • Hydroxylamine is then used to reduce back to Pu(III), and this is repeated a few times to reduce contamination.
      • Finally, Pu oxalate is formed and separated using a rotary vacuum filter, before PuO2PuO_2PuO2​﻿ is produced by calcination under argon.
      • Note that this purification process also produces gaseous products with similar composition to the dissolver off-gas.
        • This undergoes caustic scrubbing and discharge after electrostatic precipitation.
    • For U purification, the organic stream is washed with more reductant and nitric acid, and the nitric acid concentration greatly decreased.
      • The Uranyl nitrate solution is then concentrated by evaporation and is converted to UO3UO_3UO3​﻿ by thermal de-nitration.
    • In both of these processes, the washing and purification steps give rise to medium active liquors.
      • If the MAL is salt-free (as in THORP), it is concentrated by evaporation, with the recovered nitric acid being recycled. The concentrate is then mixed with HAL.

Other Waste

  • Low activity liquid wastes containing no actinides are discharged to sea after monitoring (this waste comes primarily from uranium purification).
  • Those low activity liquid wastes which do contain actinides are treated to precipitate out the actinides and other fission products as a ferric floc (iron coagulant used to remove suspended impurities).
    • This waste comes primarily from plutonium purification.
    • Following filtration, this liquor joins other LAL in discharge, and the concentrated floc is encapsulated in cement for deep repository storage.
  • Low activity solid waste is mostly general rubbish like gloves.
    • This is placed in metal drums, compacted, and stored in an engineered landfill site.
  • Spent solvents are radiolitically and chemically degraded, so must be replaced periodically, with spent solvent being decomposed by alkaline hydrolysis (NaOH).

Engineering Factors

  • Systems must be designed for maintenance and operation in extreme environments.
    • E.g. may maintain pumps by replacing them within an inset housing.
  • Radioactivity requires biological protections, remote maintenance, results in the degradation of materials, makes video inspection difficult (radiation levels can destroy capacitors in cameras), and can lead to radiolysis (c.f. electrolysis).
  • Plutonium must be accounted according to IAEA safeguards to prevent nuclear weapon proliferation.
  • Corrosive chemicals are handled by using simple designs with minimal moving parts, along with high grade stainless steels.
    • Most systems work by gravity flow to reduce the need for pumps.
