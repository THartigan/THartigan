L9 - Nuclear Fuel Management

Out-Of-Core Fuel Management

  • This concerns when to refuel a reactor, what fuel to load and how much fuel to load.
  • A reactor requires refuelling when reactivity reaches zero.
    • There is an approximately linear relationship between reactivity and burn-up for the slightly enriched fuel used in AGRs and LWRs.
    • For Magnox or CANDU reactors using unenriched uranium fuel, the characteristic is more non-linear.
    • Typical values for burn-up when the fuel is removed from a reactor (discharge bun-up) are (in MWd/kg); Magnox: 5-7, AGR: 24-27, LWR: 50-60 and LMFBR: 100-200.

On-/Off-Line Refuelling

  • Online refuelling allows for constant power generation and increases average fuel burn-up according to equation (2). However, it also increases the probability of an accident occurring whilst the reactor is at power.
  • Most reactors (PWRs and BWRs) are refuelled offline, and maintenance occurs simultaneously, taking around a month. Hence, the more often these reactors are refuelled, the less electricity they generate, reducing the incentive to increase burn-up.
    • Most LWRs are operated on a 3 or 4 batch strategy.
    • Refuelling generally occurs during periods of low electricity demand (summer in Europe, spring / autumn for locations with high air conditioning demands).

In-Core Fuel Management

  • This concerns the placement of new fuel and whether to rearrange old fuel.
  • In-core fuel management is only really practical for reactors which are refuelled offline.
    • In such reactors, the loading pattern (LP) must be designed to control local spatial effects from the arrangement of the fuel, such as hot-spots if too much new fuel is clustered together.

Loading Pattern Design

  • It is standard practice to maintain quarter-core symmetry (either reflective or rotational), so for the 193 fuel channels in a PWR, there are 104310^{43}1043﻿ possible LPs.
    • The optimal core design would limit the magnitude of local power peaking, limit neutron leakage (reduce irradiation damage to the pressure vessel), maximise average burn-up and maximise overall reactivity of the core (reduces amount of fresh fuel required).
    • This is often achieved manually by considering that:
      • Clusters of assemblies of a given type result in severe power peaking, so checker-boarding is essential.
      • Placing fresh fuel in the interior reduces neutron leakage (but generates more power peaking)
      • Placing fresh fuel at the edge of the core gives the flattest power profile, but high leakage
      • Low leakage reduces the amount of fresh fuel required, increasing average burn-up per cycle.

Burnable Poisons (BPs)

  • These are neutron absorbers (normally gadolinia, Gd2O3Gd_2O_3Gd2​O3​﻿ pins) whose effect in reducing fuel reactivity decreases as neutrons are absorbed.
    • Altering the number of pins and the gadolinia loading in each pin determines the length of time for the poison to be burnt out, enabling different reactivity-burn-up characteristics, with the preferred configuration being that which holds the reactivity of fresh fuel constant over its first cycle.
    • As the BPs reduce the flux, they can be used to overcome power peaking problems in otherwise desirable core designs.
