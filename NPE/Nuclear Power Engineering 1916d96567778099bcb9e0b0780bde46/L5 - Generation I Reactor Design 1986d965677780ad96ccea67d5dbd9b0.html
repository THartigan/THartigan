L5 - Generation I Reactor Design

  • Most reactors are built to generate power, but some large ones are built for plutonium production and many small ones are built for isotope generation, research or prototypes.

Reactor Types

Common Features

  • The core consists of fuel and moderator.
    • Coolant flows through gaps or annular channels in a solid moderator like graphite .
    • Liquid moderators like H2OH_2OH2​O﻿ or D2OD_2OD2​O﻿ can either act directly as a coolant by circulating around the core, or coolant may flow in tubes between the fuel and moderator.
    • The core is enclosed within a pressure vessel (normally steel) supporting pressures up to around 150 bar in water-cooled reactors or 40 bar in gas-cooled reactors.
      • Steel-lined prestressed concrete pressure vessels may also be used, doubling up as the biological shield (containment)
  • The biological shield surrounds the reactor and prevents the release of γ\gammaγ﻿-radiation and neutrons - often a several foot thick concrete vessel.
    • In submarine reactors where space is limited, a combination of steel and water is used.
  • Reactors can be designed to refuel whilst online with access to fuel elements provided to the charge machine from above via standpipes in the shield.
    • Other reactors must be taken offline for refuelling by removal of the pressure vessel cover.
  • Control rods are normally positioned above the reactor (so fall into the reactor under gravity in the case of power loss).
    • Control rods are often made of boron carbide, B4CB_4CB4​C﻿ due to Boron’s high neutron cross-section, but hafnium, cadmium, or a silver-indium-cadmium mix can also be used.
  • The coolant normally flows up through the core, being circulated through the core and heat exchangers by pumps or blowers.
    • There are normally redundant cooling loops to allow for maintenance.
  • Steam is used as the working fluid, passing through a turbine, condenser and pump.
    • The reactor coolant exit temperature is relatively low (300-400∘C300\text{-}400^\circ C300-400∘C﻿), limiting exit temperature from heat exchangers and therefore thermodynamic efficiency.

Magnox Reactors

  • These were the first power reactors built in Britain.
  • Graphite moderated and CO2CO_2CO2​﻿ cooled, fuelled with natural uranium metal in finned magnox (magnesium non-oxidising - a magnesium-aluminium alloy) cladding. Used B4CB_4CB4​C﻿ control rods.
    • Magnox has a low neutron cross-section, enabling use of natural uranium fuel.
    • Here, maximum coolant temperature is limited by the melting point of the magnox cladding around 410∘C410^\circ C410∘C﻿ - limiting thermodynamic efficiency to around 31%31 \%31%﻿.
