Effects of Radiation

  • Very rarely, radiation damages DNA directly, but often the dominant process is via indirect chemical damage, such as through the creation of free radicals - mostly reactive oxygen species (ROS).
    • Note that radiation is only one of many methods by which such free radicals are produced and the body has defences to mitigate their damage. The limited capacity of these defences leads to dose rate impacting the effects of exposure.
  • There are two types of radiation effects;
    • Non-stochastic effects which are directly observable and their severity correlated with the dose.
      • Exposure up to 0.5 Sv has no obvious effect, up to around 2.5 Sv causes radiation sickness, with higher exposure carrying a risk of death, which becomes around 100% at 6 Sv.
    • Stochastic effects - where the risk is correlated with the dose but the severity isn’t, such as the risk of developing cancer.
      • Difficult to measure at low doses, but there are no observable health effects despite the fact that background levels vary up to 100×100\times100×﻿.
      • Current regulations, however, assume a linear no-threshold (LNT) approach (any dose is dangerous) and ignore dose rate dependence.

Dose Limits

  • Dose limits are on the LNT assumption and exposure for the public should be kept below 0.5 mSv/yr or for workers, 20 mSv/yr, giving cancer risks according to a LNT of around 1/160,000 and 1/20,000 respectively.
    • UK natural background is around 2 mSv/yr.
