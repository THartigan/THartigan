Compound Nucleus Formation

  • The absorption of a neutron by a nucleus forms a compound nucleus in an excited state. The excited state then decays immediately, either emitting a particle, γ\gammaγ﻿-radiation, or both. The decay products delineate types of compound nucleus interaction;
    • Capture: The compound nucleus decays to its ground state by emission of only γ\gammaγ﻿-radiation;
    • (n,α)\boldsymbol{(n,\alpha)}(n,α)﻿ Interaction: The excited compound nucleus decays by α\alphaα﻿ emission;
    • Inelastic Scattering: The excited compound nucleus decays by emitting a neutron, and the resulting nucleus is still excited such that it then also emits γ\gammaγ﻿-radiation;
      • Note, kinetic energy is not conserved, as some of the incident neutron kinetic energy is lost as γ\gammaγ﻿-radiation.
    • Compound Elastic Scattering: The excited compound nucleus decays by emitting a neutron, and the resulting nucleus is in its ground state;
      • Here, kinetic energy is conserved, and we note this process is indistinguishable from potential scattering. Hence, both processes are known collectively as elastic scattering.
    • Fission: In heavy elements, the excited compound nucleus can decay into two intermediate nuclei and a few neutrons.

Cross-Section Dependence on Neutron Energy

  • Neutrons released in fission reactions with energy around 2 MeV2 \text{ MeV}2 MeV﻿ are known as fast neutrons. These have relatively small fission cross-sections.
    • The fission cross-section for fissile isotopes is largest for neutrons with energies around 0.02 eV0.02\text{ eV}0.02 eV﻿, which are known as thermal neutrons, as 0.02 eV≈kT0.02\text{ eV}\approx kT0.02 eV≈kT﻿ for T≈300 KT\approx 300\text{ K}T≈300 K﻿.
    • A reactor must therefore slow neutrons (moderate them). This is primarily achieved through elastic scattering.
  • Elastic scattering cross-sections for light elements are approximately independent of neutron energy up to around 1 MeV1 \text{ MeV}1 MeV﻿. Due to the rapid energy transfer to light elements described by (1), few collisions are required for neutron thermalisation.
    • For intermediate and heavy elements, σe\sigma_eσe​﻿ is constant at low energy and varies at higher energies, but generally remains within 2 b≤σe≤20 b2\text{ b}\le \sigma_e\le 20 \text{ b}2 b≤σe​≤20 b﻿.
    • Notably, water and heavy water do not follow this rule, as their molecular structure affects the scattering of low energy neutrons, increasing σe\sigma_eσe​﻿ below 1 eV1 \text{ eV}1 eV﻿;
  • Inelastic scattering occurs mostly between high energy neutrons and intermediate-heavy elements.
    • There is often a threshold neutron energy above which σi≠0\sigma_i \not=0σi​=0﻿ - the threshold energy is very high for light nuclei (6.5 MeV6.5 \text{ MeV}6.5 MeV﻿ for oxygen) but is small for large nuclei (0.05 MeV0.05\text{ MeV}0.05 MeV﻿ for Uranium).
    • Above the threshold energy, σi\sigma_iσi​﻿ generally rises to a constant, small value around a few Barns.
