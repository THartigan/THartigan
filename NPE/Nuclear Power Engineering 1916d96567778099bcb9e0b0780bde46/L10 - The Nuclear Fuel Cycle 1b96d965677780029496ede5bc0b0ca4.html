L10 - The Nuclear Fuel Cycle

  • Currently viable fuel types include natural uranium metal (CANDU, Magnox), enriched uranium oxide (most other reactors), mixed uranium and plutonium oxide - MOX, and plutonium oxide (fast reactors).

Fuel Cycles

  • There are two types of fuel cycle, open and closed. Both follow the process below, but at the end of the closed cycle, the unused Uranium and Plutonium are recovered for reuse.
    1. Uranium is mined and purified.
    1. U235U_{235}U235​﻿ content is enriched.
    1. Uranium converted to UO2UO_2UO2​﻿ and formed into fuel elements.
    1. Fuel elements are left in the reactor for the desired burn-up.
    1. Spent fuel is moved into local cooling ponds.
    1. After sufficient local cooling, they are either transferred for long term dry storage (open cycle) or reprocessing (closed cycle).
    1. In a closed cycle, the unused Uranium and Plutonium are then recovered for reuse and the fission products are stored.
  • The closed cycle was required for magnox reactors because the integrity of the sodium alloy cladding could not be guaranteed in long-term storage. Oxide fuel cladding, does not have this issue, so economics now dictates the choice between open & closed cycles.
    • France are now the only country operating on a closed cycle, having built significant reproducing infrastructure in the 80s.
    • Reprocessing is also controversial because it produces both plutonium stockpiles and high activity waste.
      • Note, however, that the plutonium produced by civil reactors is mostly Pu-240 and Pu-241, neither of which can be easily used in nuclear weapons, but can be used as reactor fuel.
    • There are currently no full-scale disposal facilities for spent nuclear fuel.
    • The increasing price of oil and gas over the next century is expected to increase uranium cost, which may increase interest in reprocessing and using Plutonium as a fuel.

Mining, Purification and Enrichment

  • Traditional mining methods involve removing rock from the ground, breaking it up and treating it to remove minerals (often using density-based separation methods).
    • In situ leaching (ISL) involves dissolving minerals with acids and then pumping that pregnant solution to the surface to recover minerals.
  • Traditional mineral processing techniques can then be used to produce yellow cake (uranium ore concentrate, UOC) with around 80% U concentration.
    • These processing techniques often involve leaching crushed ore with sulfuric acid, separating this pregnant liquor from the barren tailings, and finally extracting uranium from this liquor using kerosene with an amine solvent.
      • Finally, the solvent is stripped using ammonium sulphate solution and gaseous ammonia. Ammonium diurinate is then precipitated from the solution and removed by centrifuge. This ammonium diurinate is then finally converted to U3O8U_3O_8U3​O8​﻿ in a furnace.
      • Reduction with hydrogen then produces UO2UO_2UO2​﻿, which can then either be smelted and cast into metal rods for natural uranium reactors, or can be converted to HEX (UF6)(UF_6)(UF6​)﻿ for enrichment.
        • UF6UF_6UF6​﻿ is advantageous, as it is gaseous at moderate conditions (64∘C)(64^\circ C)(64∘C)﻿.
      • At this stage, the material is not highly radioactive and can be transported safely.
