Supernovae

Type II

  • Massive stars M>M⊙M>M_\odotM>M⊙​﻿ eventually form iron cores which cannot undergo fusion, so the star collapses.
    • A large fraction of the energy is released as neutrons from inverse β\betaβ﻿-decay (e−+p→n+νe^-+p\rightarrow n+\nue−+p→n+ν﻿)
    • When core reaches nuclear density, the collapse bounces, ejecting matter at very high velocity and inducing an r-process.
      • In an r-process, neutrons pile into nuclei more rapidly than in weak decay, generating heavy elements.
    • Leaves a Neutron star or Black Hole.

Type Ia

  • When a White Dwarf accretes enough mass to exceed the Chandrasekhar mass, so may be used as “standard candles”.
  • Distinguishable from type II by a lack of hydrogen features in the spectrum.
  • Often around 5×5\times5×﻿ more luminous than Type II.
